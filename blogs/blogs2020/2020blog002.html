why i can't go back to android (even though i want to) pt. 1

a previous android user's perspective

Posted by Aaric Han on December 17, 2020

prologue

This is not going to be a direct "why iPhone is better than Samsung" bash fest (although it will look like it the more you read and the more I type). Instead, I want to explain my reasons for loving Samsung/Android from 2015-2019 and why I made the switch over to Apple products in the past year. I love technology and I do a ton of research on it. Calling me a ____ fanboy is probably the one of the worst insults you can give me.

I'm going to divide the discussion into many smaller sections chronologically. You will also learn about my technology habits and my workflow changes through the years. Hopefully, you can learn something from this and be more knowledgable the next time you have an Android vs. iOS debate.


a new beginning (android)

A quick preface before I begin: I owned an iPod touch 4 before high school. But the first phone I bought with my own money was a Samsung Galaxy S6 active. I'm going to start my discussion with the Samsung because the iPod is irrelevant to the topic of this blog (I barely used the iPod since it was always locked away in a filing cabinet by my mom #tigermom). Also, the S6 active and S7 Active I'm going to reference in the next parts were used phones off eBay. The problems I mention below might not be applicable for new phones purchased from the manufacturer.

I purchased my first phone (Samsung Galaxy S6 active) in 2016 because my friend Anirudh had one as well. It had a bigger and more colorful screen than iPhone. It also had three buttons instead of one which makes performing tasks like going home, opening multitasking, and going back a page in any application much much easier than on iPhone. Also he had some gnarly wallpaper and icon pack which made his home screen look really customized (read: awesome). Guess what? The phone was barely over one year old and costs $400 cheaper than the iPhone. From that day on, I worked so hard mowing the lawn and washing dishes so I could buy the Samsung. Too bad it was stolen when I went to the orthodontist. *facepalm* It was my favorite phone even to this day (I might pick one up on eBay, prices should have dropped to below $100 now). Just to be very clear. It is/was my favorite phone over any other phone I have ever owned or used.

I needed a new phone and since I was pretty happy with the Samsung Galaxy S6 active, why didn't I just get the newer version, the S7 Active? I did and it was a huge mistake. The S7 Active was slower than the S6 active and even though the battery was bigger than the S6 active, the phone died quicker than the S6 active. Nearing the end of 2018, the S7 Active overheated and slowed down while performing simple tasks and it became unusable. I then got the Galaxy S9.

The Galaxy S9 didn't have good battery life since the beginning since the screen was bigger than the S6 and S7 and the battery was smaller than both the S6 and S7. As I used it for a year, the battery life degraded to the point where it could only last me the school day (6 hours) and the new software updates slowed the phone down and removed features or functions that were available previously. I just downright hate Samsung's One UI skin on top of Android. It tried too hard to copy Apple's iOS and was no longer minimalistic and functional compared to the old Samsung skin on top of Android. It was at that point where I began to notice all the features I was missing out on. (btw, this is me in high school. I wanted to somewhat chase clout and participate in whatever trendy trend was happening at the time). I couldn't play GamePigeon games with my friends on iMessage. I couldn't see what inappropriate memes people were AirDropping to each other. It felt like I was left out of the group.

AND THAT'S EXACTLY WHAT APPLE MARKETING WANTS ME TO FEEL! (but I digress)

Even though felt like I was missing out on certain things, I couldn't give up my Super AMOLED rich, vibrant, beautiful, amazing, pretty, stunning, and gorgeous screen (and ad-free YouTube). Transferring files like audio, video, and pdf files were also so much easier to do on Samsung/Android. The expandable SD card slot also allowed me to save my ~40GB of photos and videos (mostly spam selfies from my friends which I still haven't gotten around to deleting even on my current phone) to an SD card which I can remove and load on my computers whenever I want. Those things were what kept me attached to Android for so long. It is also what I sorely miss on my current iPhone.

epilogue

I'm tired of typing so I'll finish up this topic another time. That concludes my high school years where I valued multifunctionality and customizability over the overall phone's user experience. I overlooked battery life consistency, performance, and UI because I was mainly focused on the extra unique features and convenience it brought to my life. Honestly I am grateful to have owned those flagship devices, but the flaws are apparent and it is my job as a consumer, and a potential engineer of these products, to point out the shortcomings in order for the engineers to improve the product. Many engineers and product designers are stuck in their own bubble, believing in something that their users don't care for or downright despise. We have to understand the experiences of users before designing a sequel otherwise, we might end up like Samsung. A strong brand that lost a once avid customer.
